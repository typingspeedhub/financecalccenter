What Is APR and APY?

APR and APY both describe interest — but they are used in different situations. This simple guide explains what each one means, how they work, and when you should pay attention to them.

Educational use only · Not financial advice

What Is APR?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) shows the yearly cost of borrowing money. It includes the interest rate and certain fees, expressed as a percentage.

APR is most commonly used for loans, credit cards, and mortgages. It helps you understand how expensive borrowing really is.

What Is APY?

APY (Annual Percentage Yield) shows how much money you earn on savings or investments in one year, including the effect of compound interest.

APY is typically used for savings accounts, interest-bearing accounts, and investments.

APR vs APY: The Key Difference

  • APR focuses on borrowing costs
  • APY focuses on earning interest
  • APY includes compounding; APR usually does not

Why Compounding Matters

Compounding means you earn interest on your interest. The more frequently interest compounds, the higher your APY will be.

When APR Matters Most

  • Comparing credit cards
  • Choosing between loan offers
  • Understanding total borrowing cost

When APY Matters Most

  • Comparing savings accounts
  • Evaluating long-term growth
  • Understanding compounding impact

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Comparing APR to APY directly
  • Ignoring compounding frequency
  • Looking only at interest rate, not total cost

Final Thoughts

APR and APY measure interest in different ways. Knowing which one applies — and when — helps you make smarter financial comparisons and avoid costly misunderstandings.
